Know More about NASA PACE Satellite

PACE is NASA's Plankton, Aerosol, Cloud, ocean Ecosystem mission, currently in the design phase of mission development.

Goals of PACE

PACE has two fundamental science goals:

  • To extend key systematic ocean color, aerosol, and cloud data records for Earth system and climate studies.
  • To address new and emerging science questions using its advanced instruments, surpassing the capabilities of previous and current missions.

How will PACE advance beyond previous ocean color missions?

Different types of phytoplankton exhibit distinct spectral patterns, allowing scientists to differentiate between harmful algal blooms (HAB) and beneficial ones by analyzing their spectral signatures. PACE's Ocean Color Instrument (OCI) will offer an unprecedented level of detail, enabling a deeper understanding of the health of marine ecosystems

PACE's unprecedented spectral coverage will provide the first-ever global measurements designed to identify phytoplankton community composition. This will significantly improve our ability to understand Earth's changing marine ecosystems, manage natural resources such as fisheries, and identify harmful algal blooms.

Why is understanding and protecting ocean ecosystems important?

We rely on the ocean for economic benefits, but the ocean is dynamic and constantly changing in ways we are only beginning to grasp. Events like harmful algal blooms (HABs) and El Niño can significantly impact regional fisheries. As our understanding of the ocean's response to climate change grows, the need for accurate forecasts of these processes, which could affect vital ocean industries, will become even more crucial.

Who will benefit from PACE data and its applications?

The data and applications from NASA's PACE mission will benefit a wide range of individuals and organizations, including:

  • Scientists and Researchers: They will use PACE data to study ocean ecosystems, atmospheric conditions, and climate change, gaining deeper insights into global environmental processes.
  • Fisheries and Marine Industries: By monitoring ocean health, harmful algal blooms (HABs), and water quality, PACE data will help these industries make informed decisions, protect resources, and mitigate potential economic impacts.
  • Environmental Policy Makers and Managers: PACE will provide critical data to inform policy decisions related to climate change, water quality management, air pollution control, and ocean conservation.
  • Educators and Students: PACE will offer valuable data for educational purposes, helping to foster a better understanding of environmental science and the Earth's changing systems.

How will PACE monitor ocean ecology?

The ocean's health is important for maintaining the health of the Earth – and the ocean's health starts at its smallest inhabitants, phytoplankton. By taking in carbon dioxide, these microscopic plants and algae play an active role in moving carbon from the atmosphere into the ocean. Many commercially important fisheries also depend on the timing and composition of blooms of phytoplankton. Thus, monitoring phytoplankton distribution and abundance can help us to better understand the complex systems that drive ocean ecology.
